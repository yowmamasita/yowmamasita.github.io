The Flood Control Scandal

Is the PGMN Video Worth ₱300 Million?

Franco Mabanta was arrested for allegedly demanding ₱300 million from Martin Romualdez to suppress a PGMN video exposé. Six days later, PGMN published it anyway. We fact-checked every claim. What we found raises an uncomfortable question: if most of this is already public, why did anyone think it was worth ₱300 million — and what does that say about the demand itself?

May 11, 2026

On May 5, 2026, National Bureau of Investigation agents arrested Franco Mabanta, the founder of Peanut Gallery Media Network, in an entrapment operation for allegedly demanding ₱350 million from former House Speaker Martin Romualdez — a figure later negotiated down to ₱300 million, payable in four tranches of ₱75 million each. The alleged price of silence: suppress a video exposé linking Romualdez to the flood control corruption scandal. Romualdez says it was extortion. Mabanta says it was a setup to silence journalism.

Six days later — shortly after midnight on May 11, the day after Mabanta and his co-accused posted ₱120,000 bail each — PGMN published the video anyway. "Romualdez has activated his whole machinery — paid trolls, scammer influencers, dishonest mainstream media outlets, corrupted officials — to make sure this episode never sees the light of day," PGMN wrote on Facebook. "Today PGMN will give Romualdez what he is afraid of. We are publishing." The framing was clear: this was journalism under siege. The nearly two-hour broadcast, anchored by CJ Hirro — now a "person of interest" in the extortion case — walks through COA audit reports and budget documents to argue that Romualdez presided over a systematic looting of the House of Representatives. The video is dense, specific, and full of peso figures. It names names. It cites committee records and constitutional provisions.

We fact-checked every major claim in that broadcast against public sources — news archives, official records, Senate documents, COA filings, and government websites. The goal was simple: is this material new, or is it a repackaging of what the Philippine press has already reported?

The answer, it turns out, is both — and the ratio matters.

I. What the Video Claims

The PGMN broadcast — titled "The Scandal That Should Finally END Martin Romualdez" — makes dozens of specific claims across 16 segments. They fall into four categories: political facts about committee appointments and power structures; budget figures from official documents; allegations about how money moved; and CJ Hirro's own editorial analysis connecting the dots.

We verified each against publicly available sources. Here is what we found.

II. The Claims That Are Already Public

The majority of the video's factual backbone is drawn from information that was already in the public domain — reported by mainstream outlets, documented in official records, or established through prior Senate hearings. This does not mean the claims are unimportant. But it means they are not secrets that anyone would need to pay ₱300 million to suppress.

Twelve of the video's core claims in this category are fully corroborated by mainstream Philippine media, with two more partially confirmed. These are not revelations. They are a compilation — an effective one, but a compilation nonetheless. The Enrile Christmas funds controversy is a decade old. The Jinggoy privilege speech has been in the public record since 2013. The flood control scandal has been covered by The Guardian and Al Jazeera. Even the COA accessibility claim is overstated: the video says reports are only available online through 2020, but the COA website actually hosts reports through at least 2023.

The video's value in this category is not in discovery but in assembly: it puts together, in one place and in accessible language, a structural argument about how congressional fiscal autonomy enables corruption. That is legitimate public-interest media. But it is not the kind of material that would typically warrant a ₱300-million asking price — which raises the question of how the figure was arrived at in the first place.

III. The Budget Data — Verified Against COA and GAA Records

A substantial portion of the video's most granular material — the line-by-line MOOE breakdowns, the year-over-year budget-versus-actual comparisons, the terminal leave figures, the cash-in-bank accumulation — is sourced from COA Annual Audit Reports that CJ Hirro reads on screen during the broadcast. Cross-referencing DBM budget documents, citizen watchdog analyses citing specific COA AAR page numbers, and news coverage, we were able to verify a significant number of these figures.

Duterte's chairmanship is confirmed, but attribution is misleading: he chaired the Accounts Committee, not Appropriations. The ₱15.57B starting point could not be verified from available GAA documents.

The video's core budget figures check out. The ₱18.66 billion in accumulated cash, the ₱2.58 billion traveling budget versus ₱539 million actual spending, the ₱3.5-billion annual gap in extraordinary expenses — these are real numbers from official government audits. CJ Hirro was reading from genuine COA reports.

PGMN's claim that COA reports are only available online through 2020 is incorrect — the COA website hosts reports through at least 2023, though they are password-protected Word files and require some bureaucratic navigation. Hard copies still cost ₱5 per page. PGMN did real work in reading and compiling these figures for a mass audience, but the data is not as inaccessible as the video suggests — which matters when assessing what the material was worth to anyone.

IV. The Claims Found Nowhere Else

A handful of claims in the broadcast could not be found in any public source, mainstream or otherwise.

These four claims are stated without supporting documents, named sources, or corroboration. CJ Hirro presents them as insider knowledge — but in the video, they remain assertions. The "stay out of the country" claim is the most notable because it would implicate Marcos, but it is also the least substantiated: no associates are named, no documents are shown, no timeline is given.

Eighteen claims fully confirmed against primary sources including official gazette GAA documents, six more partially corroborated, one factual error (the COA online accessibility claim), and four claims found in no public source. The budget data that CJ Hirro reads from COA reports has been independently verified against GAA documents and watchdog analyses. The ₱18.66 billion in accumulated cash, the ₱3.5-billion annual gap in extraordinary expenses, the terminal leave figures — all real.

The four unverified claims — the presidential cover-up allegation, the Land Bank figure, the 2007 promise, the internal House practices — remain unsupported. They are also the smallest part of the video, but they are arguably the most explosive — and the most likely to command a premium if someone were trying to sell, rather than publish, the material.

That ratio — overwhelmingly public information, plus a handful of unsubstantiated bombshells — is the crux of the story.

VI. So Is It Worth ₱300 Million?

Not as a collection of facts — the political appointments, the historical precedents, and the flood control scandal are already widely reported. The COA budget data is more granular and less widely known, though it is available on the COA website, not locked behind the paywall the video claims. The specific budget-versus-actual breakdowns — ₱2.58 billion budgeted for travel against ₱539 million actually spent, ₱3.5 billion in extraordinary expenses appearing from nowhere year after year, ₱18.66 billion quietly accumulating in a bank account — are real and damning. But they are also, by definition, government records that any journalist, legislator, or citizen watchdog can obtain. PGMN made these figures accessible to a large audience. That has public-interest value. Whether it has ₱300-million value is a different question.

Then there are the four unverified claims — alleged presidential involvement, the Land Bank figure, the 2007 promise, the internal House practices. These are stated without documentary proof in the video. On their own, they are assertions. If PGMN possesses evidence backing them, the picture changes — but the video does not show that evidence.

The Timeline

Mabanta was arrested on May 5. His phones were seized. CJ Hirro was subpoenaed. The NBI declared her a person of interest. PGMN framed this as "the machinery of suppression."

Six days later, after posting bail, PGMN published the video just past midnight on Mother's Day. The arrest and national news coverage drove more attention to the video than PGMN could have generated on its own. But there is another way to read the timeline: a media outfit allegedly demanded ₱350 million, was caught in an entrapment operation, and — after that leverage evaporated — published the material anyway, reframing the sequence as persecution. Publishing after arrest is consistent with journalism under pressure. It is also consistent with a failed transaction.

VII. The Uncomfortable Middle

The most troubling possibility is the one that fits the evidence most comfortably: that the PGMN broadcast is a mixture of legitimate public-interest reporting and unverified allegations — and that the extortion allegation may also be true.

These are not mutually exclusive. It is possible that PGMN genuinely investigated Romualdez's budget irregularities, that CJ Hirro did real work analyzing COA reports, that the network also possesses insider information not yet public — and that Mabanta attempted to monetize the material rather than publish it. A journalist can do good work and then make a criminal decision about what to do with it. The ₱350-million initial demand — negotiated down to ₱300 million in four tranches — has the structure of a business negotiation, not a ransom.

The alternative — that the arrest was a manufactured operation to neutralize a media threat — is possible but harder to square with the specifics. The NBI conducted a formal entrapment operation using its own funds. The ₱300-million demand was allegedly made by Mabanta directly. The plunder charges against Romualdez were filed the same day as the arrest, suggesting the political pressure was real — but also that PGMN would have known the material's leverage was about to expire. A demand for payment, if it happened, would have been most valuable before the Ombudsman acted — not after.

UP journalism professor Danilo Arao's assessment of Mabanta — that he is "not a journalist" but a "political operative" — is worth taking seriously. Mabanta served as social media director for the Marcos campaign before founding PGMN. The question is not whether the material in the video is accurate — it mostly is. The question is what Mabanta intended to do with it: publish it, or sell the suppression of it. The verified content of the video does not answer that question. The ₱350-million asking price, the negotiation, and the entrapment suggest one answer. PGMN's post-arrest publication suggests the other — or simply that Plan A failed.

If the video is largely a compilation of public information, what made anyone think it was worth ₱300 million — and who set that price?

The Romualdez camp has a straightforward answer: they wouldn't. The ₱300-million demand was the crime, not the content. Atty. Elaine Atienza's statement is precise on this point: "If anyone believes he has evidence of wrongdoing by public officials, the proper course is simple: release it, submit it to the authorities, and let the proper institutions act on it — not use it as leverage in exchange for money."

That logic is difficult to argue with — and our own findings support it. The video is overwhelmingly composed of public information. The genuinely new claims are unsubstantiated. If the goal were public accountability, the material could have been published at any time. Instead, according to the NBI, a price was set — ₱350 million, negotiated to ₱300 million — and a payment structure was proposed. The NBI's subpoena of CJ Hirro and seizure of devices are standard evidence-preservation steps in an extortion investigation, not necessarily acts of suppression. That PGMN published six days later does not disprove the extortion allegation; it may simply mean the material lost its transactional value once the arrest became public.

VIII. What's Missing

After cross-referencing every claim in the publicly available PGMN broadcast, we are left with a clear picture of what the video is and what it is not.

It is a substantively accurate, data-driven analysis of structural corruption in the House of Representatives. The budget figures CJ Hirro reads from COA reports — the ₱18.66 billion cash accumulation, the ₱3.5-billion annual gap in extraordinary expenses, the ₱2.58-billion travel budget against ₱539 million in actual spending — have been independently verified against GAA documents and COA records. With 18 confirmed claims, 6 partially corroborated, one factual error, and four unverified claims, the broadcast is largely accurate on the numbers — though its framing and editorial claims are less reliable.

It is not the kind of video that would send someone to prison on its own. The four unverified claims are stated without documentary evidence. The budget analysis is largely accurate, but the video's editorial framing — including its false claim about COA data accessibility — shows a willingness to overstate the case. Assertion is not proof.

What we cannot assess is whether PGMN possesses supporting evidence it has not yet published — documents, recordings, or witnesses that back the four new claims, particularly the allegation that Romualdez invoked presidential authority in directing associates to flee. If such evidence exists, the video is a preview of something far more consequential. If it does not, the video is what it appears to be: a well-researched compilation of mostly public information, bundled with a handful of explosive but unproven assertions.

So is it worth ₱300 million? The video is public. The data in it is largely public — more accessible than PGMN claims, and available to anyone willing to navigate the COA website. The four unverified claims are stated without evidence. What someone allegedly set as the price of suppression — ₱350 million, negotiated to ₱300 million — says less about the video's public-interest value than about what someone believed the material was worth as leverage. That gap — between what the video actually contains and what someone allegedly demanded for it — may be the most telling thing about this entire case.
