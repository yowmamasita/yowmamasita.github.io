The Flood Control Scandal

Is the PGMN Video Worth ₱300 Million?

Franco Mabanta was arrested for allegedly demanding ₱300 million from Martin Romualdez to suppress a PGMN video exposé. Six days later, PGMN published it anyway. We fact-checked every claim. What we found raises an uncomfortable question: if most of this is already out there, what exactly was Romualdez paying to keep quiet?

May 11, 2026

On May 5, 2026, National Bureau of Investigation agents arrested Franco Mabanta, the founder of Peanut Gallery Media Network, for allegedly demanding ₱300 million from former House Speaker Martin Romualdez. The price of silence: suppress a video exposé linking Romualdez to the flood control corruption scandal. Romualdez says it was extortion. Mabanta says it was a setup to silence journalism.

Six days later — shortly after midnight on May 11, the day after Mabanta and his co-accused posted bail — PGMN published the video anyway. "Romualdez has activated his whole machinery — paid trolls, scammer influencers, dishonest mainstream media outlets, corrupted officials — to make sure this episode never sees the light of day," PGMN wrote on Facebook. "Today PGMN will give Romualdez what he is afraid of. We are publishing." The nearly two-hour broadcast, anchored by CJ Hirro — now a "person of interest" in the extortion case — walks through COA audit reports and budget documents to argue that Romualdez presided over a systematic looting of the House of Representatives. The video is dense, specific, and full of peso figures. It names names. It cites committee records and constitutional provisions.

We fact-checked every major claim in that broadcast against public sources — news archives, official records, Senate documents, COA filings, and government websites. The goal was simple: is this material new, or is it a repackaging of what the Philippine press has already reported?

The answer, it turns out, is both — and the ratio matters.

I. What the Video Claims

The PGMN broadcast — titled "The Scandal That Should Finally END Martin Romualdez" — makes dozens of specific claims across 16 segments. They fall into four categories: political facts about committee appointments and power structures; budget figures from official documents; allegations about how money moved; and CJ Hirro's own editorial analysis connecting the dots.

We verified each against publicly available sources. Here is what we found.

II. The Claims That Are Already Public

The majority of the video's factual backbone is drawn from information that was already in the public domain — reported by mainstream outlets, documented in official records, or established through prior Senate hearings. This does not mean the claims are unimportant. But it means they are not secrets that anyone would need to pay ₱300 million to suppress.

Fourteen of the video's core claims are fully corroborated by mainstream Philippine media. These are not revelations. They are a compilation — an effective one, but a compilation nonetheless. The Enrile Christmas funds controversy is a decade old. The Jinggoy privilege speech has been in the public record since 2013. Concurrent Resolution No. 10 has been reported on by every major outlet. The flood control scandal has been covered by The Guardian and Al Jazeera.

The video's value in this category is not in discovery but in assembly: it puts together, in one place and in accessible language, a structural argument about how congressional fiscal autonomy enables corruption. That is legitimate public-interest media. But it is not the kind of material that would typically warrant a ₱300-million suppression payment.

III. The Budget Data — Verified Against COA and GAA Records

A substantial portion of the video's most granular material — the line-by-line MOOE breakdowns, the year-over-year budget-versus-actual comparisons, the terminal leave figures, the cash-in-bank accumulation — is sourced from COA Annual Audit Reports that CJ Hirro reads on screen during the broadcast. Cross-referencing DBM budget documents, citizen watchdog analyses citing specific COA AAR page numbers, and news coverage, we were able to verify a significant number of these figures.

The video's core budget figures check out. The ₱18.66 billion in accumulated cash, the ₱2.58 billion traveling budget versus ₱539 million actual spending, the ₱3.5-billion annual gap in extraordinary expenses — these are real numbers from official government audits. CJ Hirro was reading from genuine COA reports.

The fact that these documents are functionally hidden from the public — available online only up to 2020, costing ₱5 per page after that — is itself part of the story. PGMN did genuine investigative work in obtaining, reading, and making these figures accessible to a mass audience. The ₱5-per-page cost and bureaucratic hurdles mean that most Filipinos would never see this data otherwise.

The "stay out of the country" claim is the most notable because it would implicate Marcos, but it is also the least substantiated: no associates are named, no documents are shown, no timeline is given.

Twenty claims fully confirmed, 5 more partially corroborated, no significant factual errors found. The budget data that CJ Hirro reads from COA reports has been independently verified against GAA documents and watchdog analyses. The ₱18.66 billion in accumulated cash, the ₱3.5-billion annual gap in extraordinary expenses, the terminal leave figures — all real.

The four unverified claims — the presidential cover-up allegation, the Land Bank figure, the 2007 promise, the internal House practices — remain unsupported. But they are also the smallest part of the video. The vast majority of the broadcast is documented and verifiable.

That ratio is the crux of the story.

VI. So Is It Worth ₱300 Million?

Not as a collection of facts — the political appointments, the historical precedents, and the flood control scandal are already widely reported. But the verified COA data tells a different story. The specific budget-versus-actual breakdowns — ₱2.58 billion budgeted for travel against ₱539 million actually spent, ₱3.5 billion in extraordinary expenses appearing from nowhere year after year, ₱18.66 billion quietly accumulating in a bank account — these are not widely known. They come from official documents that cost money to obtain and that most Filipinos will never see. The video's achievement is not just assembly; it is making government audit data accessible and damning to an audience of hundreds of thousands.

Then there are the four unverified claims — alleged presidential involvement, the Land Bank figure, the 2007 promise, the internal House practices. These are stated without documentary proof in the video. On their own, they are assertions. If PGMN possesses evidence backing them, the picture changes — but the video does not show that evidence.

The Streisand Effect

Mabanta was arrested on May 5. His phones were seized. CJ Hirro was subpoenaed. The NBI declared her a person of interest. The machinery of suppression — as PGMN would later call it — moved with speed and force.

Six days later, after posting bail, PGMN published the video just past midnight on Mother's Day. "The story they could not kill is now the story of how hard they tried to kill it," they wrote. The arrest, the entrapment operation, the national news coverage — all of it drove more attention to the video than PGMN could have generated on its own. If the goal was suppression, the operation achieved the opposite.

VII. The Uncomfortable Middle

The most troubling possibility is the one that fits the evidence most comfortably: that the PGMN broadcast is a mixture of legitimate public-interest reporting and unverified allegations — and that the extortion allegation may also be true.

These are not mutually exclusive. It is possible that PGMN genuinely investigated Romualdez's budget irregularities, that CJ Hirro did real work analyzing COA reports, that the network also possesses insider information not yet public — and that Mabanta attempted to monetize the material rather than publish it. A journalist can do good work and then make a criminal decision about what to do with it.

It is equally possible that the arrest is exactly what PGMN says it is: a manufactured operation to neutralize a media threat during a politically critical window. The timeline supports this reading. The NBI's own money was used in the sting. Romualdez was facing imminent plunder charges. A media outfit with 668,000 followers was about to publish a video connecting him to the scandal. The arrest came, the phones were seized, and the anchor was subpoenaed. Six days later, PGMN published anyway.

UP journalism professor Danilo Arao's assessment of Mabanta — that he is "not a journalist" but a "political operative" — may be accurate. It may also be irrelevant. The question is not whether Mabanta is a journalist. The question is whether the material in the video is accurate — and on that point, the evidence is clear: it mostly is. The question after that is why, if the video is largely a compilation of public information, anyone would go to the trouble of an NBI entrapment operation over it.

If the video is just a compilation of public information, why would anyone pay ₱300 million — or orchestrate an NBI operation — to suppress it?

The Romualdez camp has a ready answer: they wouldn't. The ₱300-million demand was the crime, not the content. Atty. Elaine Atienza's statement is precise on this point: "If anyone believes he has evidence of wrongdoing by public officials, the proper course is simple: release it, submit it to the authorities, and let the proper institutions act on it — not use it as leverage in exchange for money."

That logic is sound. But it does not address the content of the video. And it does not explain why the NBI simultaneously moved to subpoena the journalist who made it, seize the devices it was stored on, and declare the anchor a person of interest — actions that appeared designed to prevent the video from ever being released. That they failed — PGMN published six days later — only sharpens the question.

VIII. What's Missing

After cross-referencing every claim in the publicly available PGMN broadcast, we are left with a clear picture of what the video is and what it is not.

It is a substantively accurate, data-driven analysis of structural corruption in the House of Representatives. The budget figures CJ Hirro reads from COA reports — the ₱18.66 billion cash accumulation, the ₱3.5-billion annual gap in extraordinary expenses, the ₱2-billion travel budget against ₱539 million in actual spending — have been independently verified against GAA documents and COA records. With 20 confirmed claims, 5 partially corroborated, and no significant errors, the broadcast is largely accurate. The video's achievement is in obtaining official audit data that is functionally hidden behind paywalls and bureaucratic hurdles, and making it accessible to a wide audience.

It is not the kind of video that would send someone to prison on its own. The four unverified claims are stated without documentary evidence. No significant factual errors were found in nearly two hours of budget analysis, but assertion is not proof.

What we cannot assess is whether PGMN possesses supporting evidence it has not yet published — documents, recordings, or witnesses that back the four new claims, particularly the allegation that Romualdez invoked presidential authority in directing associates to flee. If such evidence exists, the video is a preview of something far more consequential. If it does not, the video is what it appears to be: a well-researched compilation of mostly public information.

So is it worth ₱300 million? The video is public. The data in it is largely public. And yet someone allegedly thought it was worth paying that much to suppress. That gap — between what the video actually contains and what someone was apparently willing to pay to bury it — is the most interesting thing about this entire case.
